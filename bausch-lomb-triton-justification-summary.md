## 4. The Problem: Why Bausch + Lomb Needs CT Scanning

### Root Cause Issue
Bausch + Lomb's surgical ophthalmic cutter products contain injection-molded plastic caps with **voids and porosity defects** from the molding process. These voids cause the **barb** (a critical component that connects tubing to the cutter body) to **fracture and detach**, either during assembly, sterilization, or shipping.

### Specific Defect Description
- The cap is a small (~1 inch cube) injection-molded component
- Voids form in the "shoulder area" of the cap during molding
- These voids cause the barb to detach from the tubing — a "skinning" effect
- The detachment can happen inside sealed sterile packaging
- When surgeons open the sterile pack and peel the Tyvek lid, the cutter is already broken
- Some failures present as cracks that lead to air suction sounds during surgery

### Scale of the Problem
- **Defect rate estimate:** 2%–5% across product lines
- **Existing inventory at risk:** ~2,400 finished sterile packs (at time of trial start), ~1,800 packs remaining
- **Incoming cap volume:** 30,000–50,000 individual caps from supplier
- **Production volume:** ~2,200–2,500 individual sterile packs per batch
- **Complaint history:** Active customer complaints on existing product lines dating back years

### Business Impact
- **Product launch at risk:** A new cutter product was scheduled for mid-March 2026 launch. Commercial team stated they "cannot afford to launch a new product with the same problem" given existing complaints on current lines.
- **Surgeon/patient safety risk:** Broken cutters cause delays in surgery (the cutter is used to cut fluid from the eye during ophthalmic procedures). While not directly dangerous to patients, it forces surgery delays.
- **Reputational damage:** Bausch + Lomb's commercial team was already managing unhappy surgeons due to existing product line failures. Launching a new product with the same defect would severely damage their credibility.
- **Recall risk:** Unscreened inventory could result in field failures and potential recall costs.
- **Financial impact estimates (order of magnitude):**
  - Direct defect costs: ~$18,000
  - Field/operational impact: Up to $288,000
  - Launch delay opportunity cost: $100K to $1M+
  - Loss of future sales from damaged surgeon relationships: Difficult to calculate but significant

## 6. The Triton Justification Case

### Why Triton Is Needed Beyond Neptune

The Neptune trial is proving the *feasibility* of CT-based inspection. However, the long-term supplier quality program requires the **Triton** for several reasons:

#### 1. Throughput Requirements
- Bausch + Lomb has **30,000–50,000 individual caps** to screen from their supplier
- They produce **thousands of cutters per month** on an ongoing basis
- Neptune is a manual, cabinet-based system — scan, open door, swap parts, close, scan
- Triton has **automated material handling** (conveyor-based pass-through), enabling continuous scanning without manual intervention
- Todd Goldstein's initial inquiry specifically asked about "inline" scanning capability

#### 2. Supplier Quality Program (Upstream Screening)
- The ultimate goal is to push inspection **upstream to the supplier** in Oakland, California
- B+L wants the supplier to scan **every cap** before assembly to ensure no voids
- This requires high-volume, repeatable, automated inspection — Triton's core capability
- From the onboarding call: "Our whole intent is to go upstream... and that's where we want the ultimate positive results so that we know what gets assembled is right"
- Ian Kelsey: "We'd like to push it back to the vendor and say, you need to screen the caps"

#### 3. Automated Recipe-Based Analysis
- Triton supports automated recipe analysis to identify defects at scale
- Once a recipe is developed (what a void looks like, where to look, accept/reject criteria), it can run unattended
- Neptune requires human-in-the-loop review for each scan
- For 50,000 caps, human review is impractical; automated pass/fail is essential

#### 4. On-Premise Server Capability
- Bausch + Lomb has strict IT security requirements (Elaine Whited raised concerns)
- The current trial is limited by WiFi upload speeds (100 Mbps)
- Triton can be deployed with an **on-premise server**, eliminating cloud upload bottleneck
- This addresses both security concerns and throughput limitations

#### 5. Multiple Facility Deployment
- Ian Kelsey discussed future-proofing: "We've got a few other facilities at play that might become more consistent high volume"
- B+L's supplier is in Oakland, CA; main facility is in St. Louis, MO; distribution is in Tennessee/Kentucky
- Lumafield's model supports adding hardware units at ~$39,500/year each under the same platform
- The Triton at the supplier + Neptune at B+L could share data through Voyager cloud

#### 6. Resolution & Speed Trade-offs
- Current Neptune can do 1-minute scans for gross failures but needs 3–5 minutes for detailed void analysis
- Triton's higher throughput means even at longer scan times, daily part counts are significantly higher
- Critical for meeting production schedules without creating bottlenecks

### Triton Deployment Scenarios Under Discussion

**Scenario A: Triton at Supplier (Oakland, CA)**
- Supplier scans all incoming caps before assembly
- Automated pass/fail based on void detection recipe
- Eliminates the defect at the source
- Supplier bears some operational responsibility

**Scenario B: Triton at B+L St. Louis (Tree Court)**
- B+L screens cutters and caps in-house before assembly
- Full control over the inspection process
- Proximity to assembly line for rapid feedback

**Scenario C: Phased Approach**
- Keep Neptune at B+L St. Louis for pack/pouch screening and ad-hoc inspection
- Deploy Triton at supplier for high-volume cap screening
- Share data through Voyager platform across sites

## 9. Triton-Specific Justification Points

### Business Case Arguments

1. **Defect Prevention at Source:** By deploying Triton at the supplier's facility in Oakland, B+L can ensure 100% incoming cap quality, eliminating the root cause rather than catching failures downstream.

2. **Cost of Inaction:** With a 2–5% defect rate on 30,000–50,000 caps, hundreds of defective units could enter the assembly process per batch. Each defective assembled product costs labor, materials, sterilization, and packaging — all wasted.

3. **Recall Avoidance:** Defective sterile-packaged products that reach the field risk costly recalls. A single recall in the medical device space can cost millions in direct costs and brand damage.

4. **Throughput Gap:** Neptune's manual process caps throughput at ~71 parts/day for full scans. At this rate, screening 50,000 caps would take ~700 days — obviously impractical. Triton with automated material handling can screen orders of magnitude faster.

5. **Regulatory Compliance:** As a medical device manufacturer, B+L must demonstrate process controls. Automated CT inspection with documented recipes and traceable results supports regulatory filings and audits.

6. **Multi-Site Scalability:** The Voyager platform model means adding Triton units at additional facilities is cost-effective ($39,500/year per additional unit). Data is shared across all sites.

7. **Surgeon Satisfaction:** Eliminating defective cutters from reaching operating rooms directly improves surgeon experience and protects B+L's market position.

### ROI Framework

| Cost Category | Without Triton (Annual) | With Triton (Annual) |
|---|---|---|
| External CT scanning service | $50K–$100K+ (estimated) | $0 |
| Defective assembled products (labor + materials) | $100K–$500K (estimated at 2-5% defect rate on high-volume production) | Near $0 (defects caught at source) |
| Field failures & complaints | $200K–$500K (estimated) | Near $0 |
| Potential recall costs | $1M–$10M risk | Mitigated |
| Surgery delays / brand damage | Unquantifiable but significant | Eliminated |
| **Triton annual subscription** | N/A | ~$150K–$250K/year (estimated) |
